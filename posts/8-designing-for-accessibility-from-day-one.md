One in four adults has some form of disability. When we design only for ourselves, we're excluding a quarter of our users. The good news: accessible design is often just good design.

## Color Contrast Matters

Low-contrast text is the most common accessibility failure on the web. Gray text on a white background might look elegant, but it's unreadable for users with low vision. Aim for at least a 4.5:1 contrast ratio for body text — tools like WebAIM's contrast checker make this easy.

## Keyboard Navigation

Not everyone uses a mouse. Every interactive element must be reachable and operable with a keyboard. If you can't tab through your entire site and use every feature, someone is locked out. Visible focus indicators are non-negotiable.

## Semantic HTML is Free Accessibility

Using `<button>` instead of `<div onclick>`, `<nav>` instead of `<div class="nav">`, and proper heading hierarchy gives screen reader users a meaningful page structure at zero cost.

Accessibility isn't charity — it's empathy encoded into pixels. And the users you help will notice and appreciate it.
